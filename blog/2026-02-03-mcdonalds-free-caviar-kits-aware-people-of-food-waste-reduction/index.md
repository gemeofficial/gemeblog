## The Other Side of the Coin: How Big Business Manages Food Waste

Before we look in our own trash bins, it’s useful to see how a giant like McDonald's approaches this problem on a global scale. The goal is to minimize waste from the start. [McDonald's has reported wasting "less than 1% of its edible food stock"](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2018/02/mcdonalds-has-promised-100-renewable-packaging-by-2025/).

How do they aim for this? Their framework is actually adapted from a widely respected source: [**the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency's (EPA) Food Recovery Hierarchy**](https://catalog-next-dev-datagov.app.cloud.gov/dataset/recipients-of-excess-food-by-county-us-and-territories-2023-epa-region-9). This is a prioritized guide for managing food waste, and it’s a great model for anyone to understand.

![McDonald's Free McNugget Caviar Raises Food Waste Concerns](./image/mcdonalds-free-mcnugget-caviar.jpg)

### The EPA's hierarchy lists the most-to-least preferred actions:

 1. **Source Reduction**: Don't create waste in the first place. For a restaurant, this means precise supply chains and production.
 2. **Donation**: Give extra, safe food to people in need.
 3. **Animal Feed**: Divert suitable scraps to feed animals.
 4. **Industrial Uses**: This includes composting and anaerobic digestion, which create energy or soil products.
 5. **Landfill/Incineration**: The last resort.

[McDonald's applies this by first trying to reduce overproduction, then donating ingredients (like during the COVID-19 pandemic, when they donated $12 million in food)](https://mcdonaldsblog.in/2025/09/on-world-environmental-health-day-we-renew-our-commitment-to-replace-recycle-reuse-reduce/page/3/). For waste that can't be avoided or donated, they look to options like composting.

For you and me at home, this hierarchy is just as relevant. Donating homemade food is tricky, and we don’t have pigs to feed. Often, our only convenient option feels like the bottom of the pyramid: the trash can, headed for the landfill. This is where the core of the problem lies for households.

## The Home Kitchen Dilemma: Why Our Food Scraps Are a Problem

The issue isn't just about being wasteful of money. When food scraps go to a landfill, they decompose without oxygen. [**This process generates methane, a potent greenhouse gas that is a major contributor to climate change**](https://www.geme.bio/blog/geme-terra-2-debuts).

The scale is enormous. In the United States, billions of pounds of food go uneaten each year. While big companies have systems to manage their waste, our household scraps are fragmented across millions of homes, making them harder to tackle collectively.

Many environmentally conscious people try backyard composting. It's a fantastic solution—if you have the space, the time, and the patience. It requires a balance of materials, regular turning, and can take months. For apartment dwellers or those with small yards, it's often not a practical option. This gap between good intention and convenient action is where technology is starting to step in.

## The Rise of the "Kitchen Composter": A Solution or a Shortcut?

Enter the new category of electric kitchen composters. Promising to eliminate odors and turn scraps into "dirt" overnight, they seem like a dream for the urban eco-warrior. However, there's a crucial catch that many buyers discover too late.

:::note
[**Most popular countertop appliances are not composters. They are dehydrators and grinders**.](https://www.geme.bio/blog/5-best-kitchen-composters-in-2026)
:::

They use high heat to dry out and chop up your food waste. The output is a preserved, shelf-stable material that has not undergone true biological decomposition. As one leading brand explicitly states, its output ["**Food Grounds aren't compost**"](https://www.geme.bio/blog/geme-vs-mill-composter-2026). This material often needs to be mixed into a traditional compost pile or buried in soil to finish breaking down, which defeats the purpose of an "all-in-one" kitchen appliance.

This is the key distinction for anyone wanting to truly close the loop at home:

 - **A Dehydrator/Grinder**: Preserves and shrinks waste. Output is dry, ground-up food.

 - **A True Composter**: Uses microbes to biologically break down waste. Output is soil-ready, nutrient-rich compost for plants.
